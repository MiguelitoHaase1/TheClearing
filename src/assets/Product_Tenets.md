# Product Tenets

These are the core design tenets for the RWE patient product. They describe *how the product should feel and behave* — not features, but principles that every feature decision should be measured against.

---

## The Two Sides

The product has two distinct halves, and every tenet serves one or both:

1. **Your Profile** — Who you are. What you fill in, what integrates, what you respond to. This is the data-capture side: surveys, device connections, health inputs. It should feel like building a picture of yourself, not filling out forms.

2. **Your Experiences** — What the product builds *for* you. These are personalized mini-apps within the product that use your data, your preferences, and your context to create something that fits you. This is the lovable side — the reason people come back.

The profile feeds the experiences. The experiences reward the profile. That loop is the product.

---

## Tenet 1: Three-Question Onboarding

Onboarding asks a maximum of three questions. After those three, the user should feel a sense of completeness — they've done something, they've started, the product already works for them.

If we need more, the product unfolds three more questions at a time. Each set of three feels like a small, optional expansion — not a mandatory continuation. The user should never feel like they're stuck in a funnel. They should feel like they're *choosing* to go deeper.

**The test:** Can someone complete onboarding in under 90 seconds and still feel like the product knows them?

## Tenet 2: Persona-Based Profiling with Immediate Payback

When a user responds to questions, they don't just submit data into a void. The product reflects back immediately — a persona, a name, an adjective, a profile that feels personal and shareable.

This is the reciprocity mechanism at the individual level: you give us data, we give you *yourself* back in a way that feels exciting. The persona should be something users want to screenshot, share, or talk about. It turns data capture into self-discovery.

**The test:** Would a user voluntarily show their profile to a friend?

## Tenet 3: Trust Through Ownership

The product earns trust not through privacy policies or compliance badges, but through a simple, visible promise: this is your data and it stays your data.

Users share data with us because they *choose* to — and they can see exactly what they're sharing, when, and why. The trust model is transparency, not authority. We are not a health system demanding information. We are a tool that works better the more you put in, and you control the dial.

**The test:** Can a user explain our data model to someone else in one sentence?

## Tenet 4: Experiences, Not Features

The second side of the product — your experiences — should feel like small, self-contained apps that were built *just for you*. They take your profile data and your preferences and create something personal: a dashboard, a nudge, a visualization, a challenge, a reflection.

Users don't configure these manually. The product assembles them from what it knows. This is the "software-for-one" principle applied at the experience layer — the product adapts to you, not the other way around.

**The test:** Do two different users see meaningfully different experiences within the first week?

---

## How to Use These Tenets

When evaluating a feature or design decision, ask:

- Does it respect the three-question rhythm, or does it add friction?
- Does the user get something back immediately, or are we just extracting?
- Does this strengthen or weaken the user's sense of ownership over their data?
- Does this feel like a personalized experience, or a generic feature?

If a proposed feature violates a tenet, it needs a strong reason to exist — or it needs to be redesigned until it doesn't.
